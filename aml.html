Anti-Money Laundering — AMLR & Wwft

The new EU Anti-Money Laundering Regulation (AMLR 2024/1624) replaces the 4th AML Directive and directly applies in all member states. In the Netherlands, the Wwft remains the primary implementing law alongside AMLR. Together they impose rigorous CDD, monitoring and reporting obligations on financial institutions.

🎯
What is Money Laundering?
Art. 2–3 AMLR / Art. 1 Wwft
▼

Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained funds appear legitimate. It typically involves three stages: placement (introducing funds into the financial system), layering (obscuring the trail) and integration (merging proceeds into the legitimate economy).

The AMLR defines a broad set of obliged entities — including banks, payment institutions, crypto-asset service providers and certain professional services firms — who must implement AML controls.

Example

A client deposits €9,500 in cash five times in one week. By staying just below the €10,000 reporting threshold, they attempt to avoid detection. This pattern — known as structuring or smurfing — is itself a criminal offence and a key red flag.

ANALYST

Watch for these patterns:

Multiple cash deposits just below reporting thresholds

Rapid movement of funds through multiple accounts

Transactions inconsistent with the client's known business

COMPLIANCE

Ensure your transaction monitoring system generates alerts for structuring patterns. Document the rationale for both filing and not filing a UTR. DNB may audit these decisions.

MANAGEMENT

The board bears ultimate responsibility for an effective AML framework. AMLR introduces direct personal liability for senior management in cases of systemic failure. Ensure AML risk appetite is explicitly documented in governance frameworks.

🔍
Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
Art. 20–34 AMLR / Art. 3–11 Wwft
▼

CDD is the cornerstone of AML compliance. You must identify and verify the client, understand the purpose and nature of the relationship, identify the UBO (beneficial owner) and conduct ongoing monitoring. CDD must be applied at onboarding and whenever risk changes.

Enhanced CDD (ECDD) applies to high-risk relationships including PEPs, high-risk jurisdictions and complex structures. ECDD requires senior management approval and more frequent reviews.

Example

A holding company applies for a business account. The ultimate beneficial owner (UBO) is a resident of a high-risk country listed by FATF. Standard CDD is insufficient — ECDD is required: source of wealth, source of funds, senior approval and enhanced monitoring must all be documented.

📊
Transaction Monitoring & UTR
Art. 50–54 AMLR / Art. 16 Wwft
▼

Obliged entities must monitor transactions and report Unusual Transaction Reports (UTRs) to the FIU-Netherlands as soon as a transaction is identified as unusual — not only when certainty exists. The threshold is reasonable suspicion.

Deadline: UTRs must be filed within 14 days of identifying an unusual transaction (or immediately for certain high-risk categories).

Tipping-off prohibition: You must not inform the client or any third party that a UTR has been filed or is being considered.

Example

A client transfers €180,000 to a cryptocurrency exchange in three transactions over five days. The client is a retired teacher with no prior investment history. This is inconsistent with their profile and must be investigated. If the suspicion cannot be resolved, a UTR must be filed.

🌍
PEPs, High-Risk Countries & Sanctions
Art. 35–40 AMLR / Art. 8 Wwft
▼

A Politically Exposed Person (PEP) is someone who holds or has held a prominent public function. PEPs always require enhanced CDD, including senior management approval and ongoing monitoring. Family members and close associates of PEPs are also in scope.

Clients or transactions connected to FATF grey- or blacklisted countries, or EU high-risk third countries, require enhanced CDD and additional controls. Always check against EU/OFAC/UN sanctions lists.

Example

A client is the spouse of a former state secretary from a high-risk jurisdiction. Enhanced CDD is mandatory: verify source of wealth, obtain senior management approval and schedule annual review. A documented business rationale for Dutch banking must exist.

⚡
Sanctions & Enforcement
Art. 56–58 AMLR / Art. 27–29 Wwft
▼

Administrative (DNB/AFM): Fines for legal entities up to €4 million or 10% of annual turnover under the Wwft. AMLR raises the ceiling to €10 million or 10% of group turnover. Enforcement decisions are published (naming and shaming).

Criminal law: Intentional money laundering by the institution or its officers can be prosecuted. Maximum 6 years imprisonment and category 5 fine (€900,000) for natural persons.

AMLA: The new EU Anti-Money Laundering Authority gains direct supervisory powers over the 40–50 largest cross-border institutions from 2028.

Enforcement Practice

DNB has issued several high-profile Wwft fines including multi-million-euro penalties for systematic CDD failures, inadequate transaction monitoring and late UTR reporting. Enforcement decisions are published at dnb.nl and afm.nl.
