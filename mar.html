What is the Market Abuse Regulation?

The MAR (EU 596/2014) is the European regulation prohibiting and combating market abuse. It applies directly in all EU member states and defines two main prohibitions: insider trading and market manipulation. In the Netherlands, the AFM supervises compliance.

🔒
Insider Trading
Art. 7–11 MAR
▼

Inside information is information that: (1) is not public, (2) is precise, (3) relates to a financial instrument or issuer, and (4) would be price-sensitive if disclosed.

It is prohibited to trade, attempt to trade, or disclose inside information (tipping).

Example

You hear from a colleague that company X will issue a profit warning next week. You sell shares in X before the announcement. This is illegal insider trading — even if you did not actively seek out the information.

TRADER

Stop immediately if you hear this:

"I heard they are about to announce something..."

Details about a merger, acquisition or earnings surprise before publication

A request to keep the information "to yourself"

👉 Do not trade. Hang up the phone. Inform compliance the same day.

COMPLIANCE

Maintain and enforce a robust insider list procedure. All persons with access to inside information must be placed on the list immediately. Chinese walls must be documented and regularly tested. Art. 18 MAR requires insider lists to be available to the AFM within 2 days of request.

MANAGEMENT

Board members must ensure that information barriers exist and are enforced. Personal liability arises if inside information flows across business lines without adequate controls. Document all governance decisions relating to inside information at board level.

📉
Market Manipulation
Art. 12–15 MAR
▼

Market manipulation covers behaviour that gives false or misleading price signals, keeps a price at an artificial level, or disseminates misleading information.

Transaction-based manipulation: Wash trading, spoofing, layering, painting the tape, ramping.

Information-based manipulation: Spreading false rumours, misleading press releases, pump-and-dump via social media.

Example — Spoofing

You place large limit orders that you immediately withdraw as soon as the price moves. The aim is to create the appearance of activity. This is spoofing and explicitly prohibited under Art. 12(2)(c) MAR.

TRADER

Red flags in client orders:

Orders placed just before market close that influence the closing price

Large orders that are quickly withdrawn (spoofing)

Transactions with no apparent economic purpose

COMPLIANCE

Surveillance systems must monitor order and transaction patterns. ESMA has identified specific patterns in its Q&As and guidelines. Ensure a documented escalation process for alerts. Not every alert is a STOR — qualitative assessment is required.

MANAGEMENT

Ensure the board receives periodic reports on surveillance findings and STOR volumes. A significant rise or fall in STORs is a material signal for the governance framework.

📋
STOR — Suspicious Transaction Reporting
Art. 16 MAR
▼

Investment firms and market operators are required to report suspicious transactions and orders to the AFM via a Suspicious Transaction and Order Report (STOR).

When: As soon as a reasonable suspicion of market abuse exists — not only after certainty. When in doubt: report.

Deadline: As soon as possible, normally within one business day of the suspicion arising.

Confidentiality: The client must not be informed that a STOR has been filed (tipping-off prohibition).

COMPLIANCE

STORs are submitted via the AFM portal. Always document the decision-making process: even the decision not to file must be reasoned and recorded. The AFM may request substantiation during an investigation.

🔊
Market Sounding
Art. 11 MAR
▼

Market sounding is the practice of informing potential investors before the announcement of a transaction. This is permitted, but only through strict procedures.

The disclosing market participant must: (1) obtain consent before starting, (2) confirm the MNPI status, (3) keep detailed records of the conversation, and (4) place the recipient in a walled garden.

⚡
Sanctions and Enforcement
Art. 30–34 MAR
▼

Administrative (AFM): Fines for legal entities up to €15 million or 15% of annual turnover. For natural persons up to €5 million. Publication of sanctions (naming and shaming).

Criminal law: Intentional conduct may be prosecuted. Maximum sentence: 6 years imprisonment and a category 5 fine (€900,000).

AFM Enforcement Practice

The AFM publishes STOR statistics and enforcement decisions annually. Enforcement cases are published via administrative decisions at afm.nl.
